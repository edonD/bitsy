# 2.5 The Science — Academic Papers, Research Findings, and GEO Methodologies

> Research task: Find, read, and summarize every relevant academic paper, industry research study, and data-driven analysis on Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), LLM citation behavior, AI search visibility, and the science behind how AI models decide what to mention and cite.

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The science of Generative Engine Optimization is built on a thin but rapidly growing evidence base. The foundational academic paper — "GEO: Generative Engine Optimization" from Princeton, Georgia Tech, The Allen Institute of AI, and IIT Delhi (2023, accepted KDD 2024) — demonstrated that specific content optimization strategies can boost visibility in generative engine responses by up to 40%, with the most effective techniques (adding quotations, statistics, and citations) being fundamentally different from traditional SEO tactics like keyword stuffing, which actually *decreases* visibility by 9%. Since that paper, a wave of large-scale industry studies has emerged: Ahrefs analyzed 17 million citations and found AI-cited content is 25.7% fresher than traditional search results; Profound analyzed 680 million citations across platforms and found only 11% domain overlap between ChatGPT and Perplexity; Yext studied 17.2 million citations and concluded "there is no single AI optimization strategy" that works across all models; and Brandlight discovered that 90% of ChatGPT citations come from pages outside Google's top 20. Meanwhile, the macro evidence is stark: Gartner predicts a 25% decline in search engine volume by 2026 and 50% decline in organic traffic by 2028; Bain found 60% of searches now end without a click; and Ahrefs showed AI Overviews reduce CTR for top-ranked pages by 58%. The science is clear — GEO is real and measurable, but most of what's marketed as "GEO strategy" has no empirical support.

## SECTION 1: THE FOUNDATIONAL PAPER — GEO: GENERATIVE ENGINE OPTIMIZATION

### 1.1 Paper Citation

**Title:** GEO: Generative Engine Optimization
**Authors:** Pranjal Aggarwal, Vishvak Murahari, Tanmay Rajpurohit, Ashwin Kalyan, Karthik Narasimhan, Ameet Deshpande
**Institutions:** Princeton University, Georgia Tech, The Allen Institute of AI (AI2), IIT Delhi
**Published:** November 16, 2023 (arXiv v1); Last revised June 28, 2024 (v3)
**Venue:** Accepted at KDD 2024 (30th ACM SIGKDD Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining)
**arXiv:** [2311.09735](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)
**ACM DL:** [10.1145/3637528.3671900](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3637528.3671900)
**Subject:** Machine Learning (cs.LG); Information Retrieval (cs.IR)

### 1.2 Abstract (Verbatim)

> "The advent of large language models (LLMs) has ushered in a new paradigm of search engines that use generative models to gather and summarize information to answer user queries. This emerging technology, which we formalize under the unified framework of generative engines (GEs), can generate accurate and personalized responses, rapidly replacing traditional search engines like Google and Bing. Generative Engines typically satisfy queries by synthesizing information from multiple sources and summarizing them using LLMs. While this shift significantly improves user utility and generative search engine traffic, it poses a huge challenge for the third stakeholder -- website and content creators. Given the black-box and fast-moving nature of generative engines, content creators have little to no control over when and how their content is displayed. With generative engines here to stay, we must ensure the creator economy is not disadvantaged. To address this, we introduce Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), the first novel paradigm to aid content creators in improving their content visibility in generative engine responses through a flexible black-box optimization framework for optimizing and defining visibility metrics. We facilitate systematic evaluation by introducing GEO-bench, a large-scale benchmark of diverse user queries across multiple domains, along with relevant web sources to answer these queries. Through rigorous evaluation, we demonstrate that GEO can boost visibility by up to 40% in generative engine responses. Moreover, we show the efficacy of these strategies varies across domains, underscoring the need for domain-specific optimization methods."

### 1.3 Methodology: GEO-Bench

The researchers created GEO-bench, the first large-scale benchmark for evaluating GEO strategies:

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Total queries | 10,000 |
| Train/Validation/Test split | 8,000 / 1,000 / 1,000 |
| Query distribution | 80% informational, 10% transactional, 10% navigational |
| Domain categories | 25 (Arts, Health, Games, Science, History, Business, Law, etc.) |
| Sources per query | Top 5 Google search results |
| Tags per query | 7 categorical (difficulty, intent, nature, domain, sensitivity, topic, answer type) |

**Data sources composing GEO-bench (9 datasets):**
1. MS MARCO (Bing search queries)
2. ORCAS-1 (Google search queries)
3. Natural Questions (research-oriented)
4. AllSouls (Oxford essay questions requiring multi-source reasoning)
5. LIMA (challenging questions requiring synthesis)
6. Davinci-Debate (debate-format questions)
7. Perplexity.ai Discover (trending queries)
8. ELI5 (Reddit complex questions needing simplified answers)
9. GPT-4 Generated (synthetically created diverse queries)

### 1.4 The Nine GEO Optimization Strategies Tested

| # | Strategy | Description | Visibility Change (Position-Adjusted Word Count) |
|---|----------|-------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Quotation Addition** | Incorporates quotations from credible sources | **+41%** (27.8 vs 19.5 baseline) |
| 2 | **Statistics Addition** | Adds quantitative data instead of qualitative discussion | **+32%** (25.9 vs 19.5) |
| 3 | **Fluency Optimization** | Improves text fluency and readability | **+28%** (25.1 vs 19.5) |
| 4 | **Cite Sources** | Adds relevant citations from credible sources | **+27%** (24.9 vs 19.5) |
| 5 | **Technical Terms** | Incorporates domain-specific terminology | **+18%** (23.1 vs 19.5) |
| 6 | **Easy-to-Understand** | Simplifies language while maintaining meaning | **+14%** (22.2 vs 19.5) |
| 7 | **Authoritative** | Makes text more persuasive and authoritative | **+12%** (21.8 vs 19.5) |
| 8 | **Unique Words** | Adds unique terms wherever possible | **+6%** (20.7 vs 19.5) |
| 9 | **Keyword Stuffing** | Adds more keywords from the query | **−9%** (17.8 vs 19.5) |

**Critical finding:** Traditional SEO keyword stuffing *decreases* visibility by 9% in generative engines. This is the single most important result for distinguishing GEO from SEO — they are not the same discipline.

### 1.5 Visibility Metrics

The paper defined two classes of visibility metrics:

**Objective Metrics:**
1. **Word Count (Imp_wc):** Normalized word count of sentences citing a source
2. **Position-Adjusted Word Count (Imp_pwc):** Word count weighted by an exponentially decaying position function — citations appearing earlier in the response count more

**Subjective Impression Metrics (7 sub-metrics, evaluated via G-Eval):**
- Relevance of cited material to query
- Citation influence on response generation
- Uniqueness of material presented
- Subjective position prominence
- Subjective content amount
- Likelihood of user clicking citation
- Diversity of material presented

### 1.6 Key Results by Search Ranking Position

Lower-ranked websites benefit *dramatically* more from GEO than top-ranked ones:

| Google Rank | Strategy | Visibility Change |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Rank 5 | Cite Sources | **+115.1%** |
| Rank 5 | Statistics Addition | **+97.9%** |
| Rank 1 | Cite Sources | **−30.3%** |
| Rank 1 | Statistics Addition | **−20.6%** |

**Implication:** GEO "levels the playing field" for smaller content creators who don't rank highly in traditional search. A page ranked #5 in Google can more than double its generative engine visibility by adding citations.

### 1.7 Domain-Specific Effectiveness

| Domain | Most Effective Strategy |
|--------|----------------------|
| Debate | Authoritative |
| History | Authoritative, Quotation Addition |
| Science | Authoritative, Fluency Optimization |
| Business | Fluency Optimization |
| Health | Fluency Optimization |
| Law & Government | Cite Sources, Statistics Addition |
| Facts/Statements | Cite Sources |
| People & Society | Quotation Addition |
| Opinion | Statistics Addition |

### 1.8 Combination Strategy Results

Combining strategies yields additional gains:
- **Fluency Optimization + Statistics Addition** showed maximum combined performance: **5.5% improvement** over best single-method approach
- **Cite Sources** averaged **31.4% improvement** when combined with other methods
- Not all combinations are additive — some strategies interfere with each other

### 1.9 Real-World Validation (Perplexity.ai)

The paper validated findings against live Perplexity.ai responses:

| Strategy | Position-Adjusted Score | Subjective Score |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Quotation Addition | 29.1 | 32.1 |
| Statistics Addition | 26.2 | 33.9 |
| Baseline | 24.1 | 24.7 |
| Keyword Stuffing | 21.9 (−10%) | — |

### 1.10 Experimental Details

- Primary engine: custom implementation using **GPT-3.5-turbo** with top-5 Google Search sources
- **5 different responses** sampled at **temperature=0.7** per query to account for stochasticity
- Real-world validation conducted on **Perplexity.ai** (commercially deployed generative search engine)
- The framework treats the generative engine as a **black box** — no access to internals; only modifies source content

### 1.11 Limitations

- Testing used GPT-3.5-based generative engines; newer models may respond differently
- GEO-bench sources limited to top 5 Google results per query
- Strategies applied uniformly, not adaptively per content type
- No longitudinal testing (single point-in-time evaluation)
- Real-world confounding factors (personalization, conversation history) not modeled
- Text-only optimization — did not affect metadata (links, domain authority)
- Did not test against Google SGE/AI Overviews, Bing Chat, or other commercial engines
- No adversarial analysis — focused on content-quality-improving strategies only

### 1.12 Follow-Up Papers Citing the GEO Work

1. **"CORE: Controlling Output Rankings in Generative Engines for LLM-based Search"** (Jin et al., 2026, [arXiv:2602.03608](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.03608v1)) — Extends GEO by targeting the synthesis stage rather than retrieval. Achieves **91.4% promotion success rate @Top-5** across GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5, Claude-4, and Grok-3. This is significant because it tests against modern models, not just GPT-3.5.

2. **"E-GEO: A Testbed for Generative Engine Optimization in E-Commerce"** ([arXiv:2511.20867](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.20867), 2025) — Adapts GEO to the e-commerce domain. Finding: GEO signals **diverge substantially** from classical SEO signals in commercial contexts.

3. **"Diagnosing and Repairing Citation Failures in Generative Engine Optimization"** — Introduces a diagnostic approach that asks *why* a document fails to be cited, rather than applying generic rewriting rules uniformly.

4. **"Beyond Keywords: Driving Generative Search Engine Optimization with Content-Centric Agents"** — Proposes an end-to-end GSEO framework addressing the obsolescence of conventional SEO metrics.

5. **Kumar and Lakkaraju (2024)** — Demonstrated that strategic text sequences can manipulate LLM recommendations to enhance product visibility (the adversarial angle that the original GEO paper intentionally avoided).

The GEO paper has also spawned the terminology ecosystem: LLMO (Large Language Model Optimization), AISO (AI Search Optimization), AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) — all variants of the same concept the paper introduced.

## SECTION 2: LARGE-SCALE CITATION BEHAVIOR STUDIES

### 2.1 Profound: 680 Million Citations Across Platforms (Aug 2024–Jun 2025)

**Source:** [AI Platform Citation Patterns — Profound](https://www.tryprofound.com/blog/ai-platform-citation-patterns)

Profound analyzed 680 million citations across ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, and Perplexity to understand how each platform sources information.

**Top-Cited Sources by Platform:**

| Platform | #1 Source | Share | #2 Source | #3 Source |
|----------|-----------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| ChatGPT | Wikipedia | 7.8% | Reddit (1.8%) | Forbes (1.1%) |
| Perplexity | Reddit | 6.6% | YouTube (2.0%) | Gartner (1.0%) |
| Google AI Overviews | Reddit | 2.2% | YouTube (1.9%) | Quora (1.5%) |

**Top-10 Source Concentration:**
- ChatGPT: Wikipedia accounts for **47.9%** of citations among top-10 sources
- Perplexity: Reddit accounts for **46.7%** of top-10 citations
- Google AI Overviews: More balanced — Reddit 21.0%, YouTube 18.8%, Quora 14.3%

**Domain Type Distribution:**
- Commercial (.com): **80.41%** of all citations
- Nonprofit (.org): **11.29%**
- Country-specific domains: ~3.5%
- Emerging tech TLDs (.io, .ai): measurable but small presence

**Cross-Platform Overlap:**
- Only **11% of domains** are cited by both ChatGPT and Perplexity
- Google AI Overviews and AI Mode cite the same URLs only **13.7%** of the time

**Platform Philosophies:** ChatGPT favors authoritative encyclopedic content; Perplexity emphasizes community-driven discussions; Google AI Overviews balances professional with social platforms.

### 2.2 Yext: 17.2 Million Citations — Model-by-Model Analysis (Q4 2025)

**Source:** [AI Citation Behavior Across Models — Yext Research](https://www.yext.com/research/ai-citation-behavior-across-models)

Yext analyzed 17.2 million distinct AI citations across Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, and SearchGPT across 7 major sectors.

**Source Control Classification:**

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Full Control | Official websites, owned blogs | Corporate sites, newsrooms |
| Some Control | Third-party directories | Google Business Profile, industry listings |
| Limited Control | User-generated platforms | Reviews, social media, Yelp |
| No Control | Independent publications | News articles, Reddit, forums |

**Model-Specific Patterns:**

| Model | Key Behavior | Full Control Range |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Gemini** | Strongest preference for official sources (E-E-A-T signals) | 22.4%–54.0% |
| **Claude** | Elevated Limited Control reliance; 2–4x higher than competitors | 6.3%–24.4% (Limited Control) |
| **Perplexity** | Most consistent behavior across sectors | 37%–50% (Full Control) |
| **SearchGPT** | Highest variance by industry | 28.2%–43.7% |

**Sector-Specific Findings:**
- **Food & Beverage:** Claude's Limited Control share at **24.35%** — nearly **10x higher** than Gemini's 2.57%
- **Healthcare:** Narrowest model divergence — Full Control ranges only 34.60%–40.45%, suggesting convergence on authoritative sources
- **Hospitality:** SearchGPT's Full Control share (~38.1%) is approximately **double** the rate of other models
- **Organizations (Non-profits):** Highest No Control rates (12.58%–18.53%)

**Key Conclusion:** "There is no single AI optimization strategy. The source mix that makes a brand visible in Gemini is not the same mix that makes it visible in Claude."

### 2.3 Profound: ChatGPT Citation Domain Concentration (Oct–Dec 2025)

**Source:** [How ChatGPT Sources the Web — Profound](https://www.tryprofound.com/blog/chatgpt-citation-sources)

Analyzed ~700,000+ U.S.-based English-language ChatGPT conversations.

**Key Findings:**
- **Top 30 domains capture 67%** of citations within a topic
- **Top 10 domains account for 46%** in product comparison topics
- Wikipedia appears in nearly **1 in 6** conversations with citations — the de facto knowledge layer
- ChatGPT **retrieved ~6x more pages than it cited** — 85% of retrieved pages were never cited
- **58% of cited URLs appeared only once** — a "wide but shallow" citation market
- Pages at Google rank #1 were cited **3.5x more often** than those beyond the top 20
- Yet **43.2%** of pages ranking #1 were cited — meaning 56.8% of #1 pages still get ignored
- **One-third of citations came from fan-out queries** (when the model decomposes queries into sub-queries)
- **95% of fan-out queries had zero search volume** in traditional keyword tools

**Content Characteristics That Drive Citations:**
- Pages of **5,000–10,000 characters** showed the biggest citation lift
- Pages above 20,000 characters averaged **10.18 citations** vs. 2.39 for pages under 500 characters
- The **10–20% section** of pages (by position) performed best across all industries
- Finance was an exception: favored shorter, denser pages

### 2.4 Brandlight: AI Citations vs. Google Rankings

**Source:** [Where AI Citations Actually Come From — Brandlight](https://www.brandlight.ai/blog/where-ai-citations-actually-come-from---and-why-traffic-isnt-the-answer)

**Headline Finding:** **90% of ChatGPT citations come from pages outside Google's top 20.**

- A domain with only 8,500 visits appeared in **23,787 citations**
- Domains with massive traffic often had minimal citations
- **Source diversity** (appearing across many different authoritative contexts) is the strongest predictor of AI citations — not raw traffic volume
- AI-Mode outputs include sidebar links about **92%** of the time
- **54% domain overlap** between AI-Mode sidebar links and top Google results (but only **35% URL overlap**)
- Average unique domains per AI-Mode answer: **~7** (2025 data)

**Consumer Trust Data (Brandlight research):**
- **41% of consumers** trust AI search results more than paid ads and at least as much as traditional organic results (2025)
- **82% of consumers** find AI-powered search more helpful than traditional search
- **6 in 10 consumers** expect to increase their use of generative AI for search tasks
- AI traffic climbed **1,052%** in financial services across 20,000+ prompts (2025)
- GEO market approached **$850 million** in 2025; **84% of respondents** recognize GEO as a concept

### 2.5 Ahrefs: 17 Million Citation Freshness Analysis

**Source:** [Do AI Assistants Prefer to Cite Fresh Content — Ahrefs](https://ahrefs.com/blog/do-ai-assistants-prefer-to-cite-fresh-content/)

Analyzed 16.975 million cited URLs across 7 platforms: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Copilot, AI Overviews, and organic Google SERPs.

**Headline Finding:** AI-cited content is **25.7% fresher** than traditional organic results.

| Platform | Average Age of Cited Content |
|----------|----------------------------|
| ChatGPT (in-text) | **1,023 days** (freshest) |
| Copilot | 1,056 days |
| Gemini | 1,118 days |
| Perplexity (in-text) | 1,166 days |
| Google AI Overviews | **1,432 days** (oldest — same as organic) |

**Citation Ordering:** Perplexity and ChatGPT show positive correlation between citation order and content age — **newer content is cited first**.

**Exception:** Google's AI Overviews cite content that is **16 days older on average** than organic results — the only AI system that doesn't favor freshness.

### 2.6 Seer Interactive: Content Recency and AI Visibility

**Source:** [Study: AI Brand Visibility and Content Recency — Seer Interactive](https://www.seerinteractive.com/insights/study-ai-brand-visibility-and-content-recency)

Analyzed 5,000+ URLs cited across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and AI Overviews.

**Content Age Distribution of AI Citations:**
- **65%** of AI crawler hits targeted content published within the past year (2025)
- **79%** targeted content from 2024–2025
- **89%** from 2023–2025
- **94%** from 2021–2025
- Only **6%** of hits were on content older than 6 years

**76.4% of ChatGPT's top-cited pages** were updated within the last 30 days.

**Practical Validation:** One Seer client saw **+300% AI traffic** after refreshing outdated content.

**Industry Variation:** Financial Services showed the largest recency bias; Travel and Energy showed less extreme freshness preferences.

## SECTION 3: AI SEARCH IMPACT — MACRO-LEVEL RESEARCH

### 3.1 Gartner: Search Volume Decline Predictions

**Source:** [Gartner Predicts Search Engine Volume Will Drop 25% by 2026](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-02-19-gartner-predicts-search-engine-volume-will-drop-25-percent-by-2026-due-to-ai-chatbots-and-other-virtual-agents)

**Published:** February 19, 2024
**Report:** "Predicts 2024: How GenAI Will Reshape Tech Marketing"
**Author:** Alan Antin, VP Analyst, Gartner

**Predictions:**
- By **2026**: Traditional search engine volume will drop **25%** due to AI chatbots and virtual agents
- By **2028**: Organic search traffic for brands could decline by **50% or more** due to generative AI search

**Supporting Survey (August 2023, 299 consumers):**
- **79%** expected to use AI-enhanced search within the next year
- **70%** expressed at least some trust in GenAI-backed search results

**Quote:** "Generative AI (GenAI) solutions are becoming substitute answer engines, replacing user queries that previously may have been executed in traditional search engines." — Alan Antin, VP Analyst, Gartner

**Current Reality Check (as of mid-2025):**
- Google handled roughly **373x more queries** than ChatGPT in 2024
- All AI-powered search tools combined made up **less than 2%** of the market
- LLM-based search accounts for about **5.6% of U.S. desktop search traffic** as of June 2025 (up from ~2.8% a year prior) — Wall Street Journal
- Top 10 AI chatbots generated **55.2 billion visits** in one year, up **80.9% year-on-year** vs. a small decline for search engines
- **Google searches per U.S. user dropped nearly 20% year-over-year in 2025** — SparkToro/Datos/Semrush report ([Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/google-searches-per-us-user-fall-report-468051))
- Apple's **Eddy Cue testified** (May 2025, DOJ antitrust trial) that **Safari searches declined for the first time in 22 years**: "That has never happened in 22 years." ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2025/05/08/apple-eddy-cue-testimony-google-alphabet-safari-ai-search-features/))
- AI search/chatbot platforms saw **721% average monthly traffic increase**, capturing nearly **8%** of combined search market by June 2025

**Skepticism on the 25% prediction** (per [Search Engine Journal](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/why-prediction-of-25-search-volume-drop-due-to-chatbots-fails-scrutiny/511270/)):
- AI chat queries cost approximately **10x more** than traditional search queries
- Microsoft's GitHub Copilot reportedly loses **$20/user/month**
- Google integrated AI Overviews directly into search, keeping users in-ecosystem
- Chatbot visits still represent roughly **3%** of total information traffic despite 80.9% YoY growth

**Inference:** Gartner's 25% by 2026 prediction appears aggressive given current data, but the directional trend is real and accelerating. The 20% per-user search decline reported by SparkToro/Datos in 2025 suggests Gartner's timeline may be closer than skeptics expected.

### 3.2 Bain & Company: Zero-Click Search Transformation

- B2B click-through rates falling by as much as **30%** in some categories, including B2B software

### 3.3 Ahrefs: AI Overviews CTR Impact

**Source:** [Update: AI Overviews Reduce Clicks by 58% — Ahrefs](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overviews-reduce-clicks-update/)

**Methodology:** 300,000 keywords from Ahrefs Keywords Explorer (150,000 with AI Overview present, 150,000 without). Compared CTR for December 2023 (pre-AI Overviews) vs. December 2025.

**Results:**
- AI Overviews reduce organic CTR for position #1 content by **58%** (updated from initial 34.5% finding in April 2025)
- Searches triggering AI Overviews show average zero-click rate of **83%** (vs. ~60% for traditional queries)
- AI Overview prevalence grew from **18.55%** of search results (Q3 2024) to **49.92%** (Q4 2025) — Advanced Web Ranking
- Being cited within an AI Overview yields **35% more organic clicks** and **91% more paid clicks** compared to not being cited

**About 93% of AI Mode searches** end without a click — more than twice the rate of AI Overviews (43%).

## SECTION 4: a16z's "GEO OVER SEO" THESIS

### 4.1 The Analysis

**Source:** [How Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) Rewrites the Rules of Search — a16z](https://a16z.com/geo-over-seo/)
**Published:** May 28, 2025
**Newsletter:** [May 2025 Enterprise Newsletter](https://a16z.com/newsletter/may-2025-enterprise/)

### 4.2 Core Thesis

> "Traditional search was built on links; GEO is built on language."

a16z argues that the shift from SEO to GEO is structural, not incremental:

| Dimension | SEO (Old Paradigm) | GEO (New Paradigm) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Success metric | Click-through rates | **Reference rates** (how often cited) |
| Optimization target | Rankings on results pages | Appearing in AI-generated answers |
| Revenue model | Ad-supported (Google) | Subscription-driven (most LLMs) |
| Market structure | Decentralized, data-adjacent | **Centralized, API-driven** |
| Query behavior | 4 words average | **23 words average** (ChatGPT) |
| Session depth | Seconds | **6 minutes average** (ChatGPT) |

### 4.3 Key Data Points

- **$80 billion+:** Current SEO market size
- **10% of Vercel signups** now originate from ChatGPT recommendations (up from 4.8% one month prior)
- ChatGPT drives referral traffic to **tens of thousands of distinct domains**
- Most LLMs are **paywalled, subscription-driven services** — "less of an incentive by model providers to surface third-party content"

### 4.4 Market Framing

a16z identifies emerging GEO measurement platforms — Profound, Goodie, and Daydream — as well as legacy SEO tools adapting (Ahrefs, Semrush, Moz, Similarweb).

**Case Study:** Canada Goose used GEO tools to determine whether LLMs spontaneously mentioned the brand — viewing this as "unaided awareness in the AI era."

**Content Optimization Insight:** "Phrases like 'in summary' or bullet-point formatting help LLMs extract and reproduce content effectively."

### 4.5 Market Opportunity

> "If SEO was a decentralized, data-adjacent market, GEO can be the inverse — centralized, API-driven, and embedded directly into brand workflows."

a16z positions GEO as having greater monopolistic potential than SEO — fewer platforms, more API-driven, more measurable.

## SECTION 5: ACADEMIC PAPERS ON LLM RECOMMENDATION BIAS

### 5.1 Cognitive Biases in LLM Product Recommendations

**Paper:** "Bias Beware: The Impact of Cognitive Biases on LLM-Driven Product Recommendations"
**Authors:** Giorgos Filandrianos, Angeliki Dimitriou, Maria Lymperaiou, Konstantinos Thomas, Giorgos Stamou
**Published:** 2025 — Accepted at EMNLP 2025
**arXiv:** [2502.01349](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.01349)

**Methodology:** Investigated how human psychological principles can manipulate LLM-based recommendation systems through modified product descriptions, treating cognitive biases as black-box adversarial strategies.

**Key Findings:**
- **Social proof bias** consistently *boosted* product recommendation rates and ranking positions
- **Scarcity and exclusivity biases** surprisingly *reduced* product visibility
- Cognitive biases are "deeply embedded in state-of-the-art LLMs, leading to highly unpredictable behavior"
- The findings pose "significant challenges for effective mitigation"

**Relevance to GEO:** Content that includes social proof signals (reviews, user counts, "trusted by X companies") may receive preferential treatment in LLM recommendations — a finding that aligns with the GEO paper's "Authoritative" strategy.

### 5.2 Position Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge

**Paper:** "Judging the Judges: A Systematic Study of Position Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge"
**arXiv:** [2406.07791](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07791)

**Key Finding:** Position bias — the tendency to favor solutions based on their position within the prompt — compromises the reliability of LLM-based evaluation. This varies significantly across models and tasks, and is strongly affected by the quality gap between solutions.

**Relevance to GEO:** When LLMs are used as recommender systems (which is what happens in AI search), the order in which candidate sources appear in the retrieval context influences which ones get cited — regardless of quality.

### 5.3 Fairness Biases in Cold-Start Recommendations

**Paper:** "Revealing Potential Biases in LLM-Based Recommender Systems in the Cold Start Setting"
**arXiv:** [2508.20401](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.20401)

**Key Finding:** State-of-the-art LLM recommender models exhibit consistent biases including gendered and cultural stereotypes, especially in cold-start scenarios (limited user history).

**Relevance to GEO:** When AI models have limited training data about a brand, default biases — toward well-known brands, certain industries, or cultural defaults — fill the gap.

### 5.4 Position Bias in LLM Recommendations

**Paper:** "Evaluating Position Bias in Large Language Model Recommendations"
**arXiv:** [2508.02020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.02020)

**Key Finding:** Items that appear earlier in the candidate list are more likely to be favored due to their position. A simple mitigation strategy: bootstrap the ranking process by ranking the candidate set repeatedly with candidates randomly shuffled at each round.

## SECTION 7: RAG ARCHITECTURE AND SOURCE SELECTION SCIENCE

### 7.1 How RAG Systems Select Sources

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is the technical architecture underlying most AI search tools. Understanding RAG is essential to understanding why certain content gets cited.

**Standard RAG Pipeline:**
1. User query → converted to embedding vector
2. Vector similarity search against document index → top-k documents retrieved (typically k=5)
3. Retrieved documents passed as context to LLM
4. LLM generates response, citing relevant retrieved documents

**Source:** [RAG for LLMs — Prompt Engineering Guide](https://www.promptingguide.ai/research/rag) and [Retrieval-Augmented Generation Survey (arXiv 2312.10997)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2312.10997)

### 7.2 Ranking vs. Selection in RAG

Recent research challenges the simple "top-k by similarity" approach:

- Traditional reranking modules score passages independently and select fixed top-K — this **struggles with complex multi-hop queries** that require synthesizing evidence across multiple documents
- Supervised retriever optimization aligns retrieval probabilities with the generator model's likelihood distribution by minimizing KL divergence
- Newer frameworks treat passage selection as a supervised learning problem, capturing **inter-passage dependencies** to dynamically select the most relevant set

**Source:** [From Ranking to Selection (arXiv 2508.09497)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.09497) and [Shifting from Ranking to Set Selection (arXiv 2507.06838)](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.06838)

### 7.3 What This Means for Content Creators

RAG systems prioritize:
1. **Semantic similarity** to the query (not keyword matching)
2. **Source authority signals** (varies by model — see Yext data in Section 2.2)
3. **Content freshness** (see Ahrefs data in Section 2.5)
4. **Content structure** — passages that can be cleanly extracted and attributed
5. **Source diversity** — RAG systems often deduplicate and diversify retrieved sources

This explains why the GEO paper's strategies work: adding citations, statistics, and quotations makes content more "citation-worthy" in a RAG context because it provides the kind of authoritative, structured, fact-dense material that LLMs preferentially surface.

## SECTION 8: CONTENT FEATURES THAT DRIVE AI CITATIONS — EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE

### 8.1 Content Length

| Content Length | Average Citations (ChatGPT) | Source |
|---------------|---------------------------|--------|
| Under 500 characters | 2.39 | Profound |
| 5,000–10,000 characters | **Biggest citation lift** | Profound |
| 20,000+ characters | **10.18** | Profound |

Finance was an exception: favored shorter, denser pages.

### 8.2 Content Position

- The **10–20% section** of pages (by vertical position) performs best across industries
- Bottom 10% of page content earns only **2.4–4.4%** of citations
- Finance: steepest distribution — **43.7%** of citations from the first 30% of content

**Implication:** Front-load the most important, citable information.

### 8.3 Content Freshness

- AI-cited content averages **1,064 days old** vs. 1,432 for organic results (**25.7% fresher**)
- **76.4%** of ChatGPT's top-cited pages updated within 30 days
- **65%** of AI crawler hits target content published within the past year
- Freshness accounts for approximately **35% of RAG weighting**

### 8.4 Source Diversity

- **90%** of ChatGPT citations come from pages outside Google's top 20 (Brandlight)
- Source diversity across the web ecosystem is the strongest predictor of AI citations — not raw traffic
- A domain with 8,500 visits appeared in 23,787 citations

### 8.5 What Doesn't Work

- **Keyword stuffing:** −9% visibility in GEO paper
- **Schema markup:** No correlation with citation rates (Search Atlas, Dec 2024)
- **llms.txt files:** No evidence of impact; no LLM crawlers observed reading them
- **Press releases:** Zero press release domains appeared in a 100-query AI category test (Loganix)

## SECTION 9: KEY RESEARCH GAPS AND OPEN QUESTIONS

### 9.1 What We Don't Know Yet

1. **Parametric vs. RAG knowledge:** When does an LLM cite from training data vs. live retrieval? No study has quantified this split for brand recommendations.

2. **Personalization effects:** Lily Ray notes LLM outputs "vary based on conversation history, user memory settings, interests, and geographic nuances." No study has controlled for these variables.

3. **Model update lag:** How long after content is published does it enter a model's citation pool? Training data cutoffs are published, but RAG index refresh rates are not.

4. **Causal GEO mechanisms:** The GEO paper shows correlation between strategies and visibility, but the causal mechanism (why does adding a quotation increase citation?) is not established.

5. **Multi-turn conversation effects:** Most studies analyze single-turn queries. In multi-turn conversations, does citation behavior change?

6. **Revenue attribution:** No peer-reviewed study has connected AI citations to revenue. Brandlight and others note this is a critical gap.

7. **API vs. UI divergence:** Most studies use API data, but "API-based tools can return different results because they bypass the retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) layer." ([Metricus](https://metricusapp.com/blog/ai-visibility-tools-worth-it-2026/))

### 9.2 Contradictions in the Literature

| Claim | Source A | Source B | Resolution |
|-------|----------|----------|------------|
| Google rank matters for AI citations | 43.2% of #1 pages cited (Profound) | 90% of citations from outside top 20 (Brandlight) | Both can be true: #1 pages are cited *more often per page* but there are far more non-top-20 pages |
| Schema markup helps | AI Mode: 4.9 vs 4.4 citations (minor positive) | ChatGPT: 3.6 vs 4.2 (negative) | Platform-dependent; no universal benefit |
| Content freshness matters | 25.7% fresher across all AI (Ahrefs) | Google AI Overviews cite 16 days *older* content | Google's AI Overviews are the exception; other AI platforms strongly favor freshness |

## SECTION 10: IMPLICATIONS FOR BITSY

### 10.1 What Bitsy Should Build (Based on the Science)

1. **Multi-model tracking is non-negotiable.** Yext's research proves "there is no single AI optimization strategy" — citation patterns differ dramatically between Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, and ChatGPT. Bitsy must test across all four at minimum.

2. **Citation accuracy detection, not just mention counting.** The biggest gap in existing tools is hallucination blindness. Bitsy should verify whether what AI says about a brand is *factually correct*, not just whether the brand was mentioned.

3. **Content-feature analysis.** The GEO paper's 9 strategies are testable. Bitsy can analyze a brand's content and score it on: citation density, statistics presence, quotation usage, freshness, content length, and front-loading of key information — then recommend specific improvements.

4. **Fan-out query detection.** Profound found 1/3 of citations come from fan-out queries with zero traditional search volume. Bitsy should test not just direct queries but decomposed sub-queries.

5. **Source diversity mapping.** Brandlight's research shows ecosystem breadth matters more than any single page's ranking. Bitsy should map where a brand appears across the web and identify gaps in coverage.

### 10.2 What Bitsy Should NOT Do (Based on the Science)

1. **Do NOT position as a "GEO" tool using that acronym.** Google's John Mueller and Lily Ray both flagged "GEO" branding as a scam signal. Position around measurable AI visibility, not buzzwords.

2. **Do NOT promise schema markup optimization as an AI visibility strategy.** No evidence supports it. Recommending it would be selling snake oil.

3. **Do NOT rely solely on API data.** The API vs. real-UI divergence is well-documented. At minimum, Bitsy should flag this limitation clearly.

4. **Do NOT use prompt-based pricing.** Every tool using credit/prompt pricing draws user complaints. Use flat-rate or usage-tier pricing.

5. **Do NOT ignore the measurement crisis.** Be transparent that AI visibility measurement is imperfect, non-deterministic, and that no tool — including Bitsy — can perfectly replicate what a real user sees.

### 10.3 Architecture Recommendations

Based on the RAG science and citation studies:
- **Test at minimum 4 models:** ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude (each has different citation behavior)
- **Run at minimum 5 samples per query per model** to account for non-determinism (informed by the GEO paper's methodology)
- **Track both mentions AND accuracy** — this is the gap every competitor misses
- **Refresh tracking at least daily** — weekly refresh is a complaint in Otterly and others
- **Include fan-out query simulation** — decompose queries the way AI models do
- **Weight freshness in recommendations** — the Ahrefs 17M-citation study proves freshness matters across all non-Google AI platforms
